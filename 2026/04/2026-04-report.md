April 2026 was characterized by a massive industrialization of software supply chain attacks and the emergence of new attack vectors targeting Artificial Intelligence ecosystems. The North Korean-nexus "Axios" compromise and the widespread "TeamPCP" campaign demonstrated how poisoning fundamental developer tools (NPM, Trivy, LiteLLM) can lead to systemic cloud credential theft, impacting over 1,000 SaaS environments and high-profile institutions like the European Commission. The month also marked a turning point in AI security, with critical vulnerabilities in multi-agent frameworks (Amazon Bedrock, PraisonAI) proving that prompt injection can escalate to full system compromise.

### PART 2 — GEOGRAPHIC SUB-SECTIONS

### Russia
Russian threat actors exhibited high operational tempo and technical sophistication this month. **Storm-1175** (affiliated with Medusa ransomware) demonstrated a remarkably short "Time-to-Exploit," weaponizing N-day vulnerabilities in web-facing assets (T1190) within 24 hours of disclosure. Meanwhile, **TA446** (Coldriver) shifted from traditional credential harvesting to mobile exploitation, deploying the "DarkSword" iOS exploit chain through spear-phishing (T1566.002) to target high-value diplomatic personnel.

### China
Activity linked to China focused on strategic espionage and systemic infrastructure access. **Salt Typhoon** successfully breached major US government surveillance and monitoring systems, highlighting a long-term interest in telecommunications and national security data. In Southeast Asia, **Operation TrueChaos** exploited a 0-day in the TrueConf client (T1543.003) to deliver the Havoc framework via DLL Side-loading (T1574.002), specifically targeting government communications.

### Iran
Iranian operations were heavily synchronized with kinetic military tensions. **Handala Hack** (Void Manticore) focused on high-impact destructive attacks, deploying wipers (T1485) against medical giants like Stryker and Israeli defense contractors. They increasingly utilize "Wiper-as-a-Service" models to lower the technical barrier for proxies. Pro-regime groups also engaged in mass DDoS and psychological operations following military strikes.

### North Korea
North Korean actors prioritized the software supply chain and decentralized finance. **UNC1069** orchestrated a sophisticated social engineering campaign against Axios maintainers, leading to the poisoning of a package downloaded 100M times weekly (T1195.002). **Lazarus** (Slow Pisces) combined long-term human intelligence (HUMINT) with malicious code commits to drain $280M from the Drift Protocol, showcasing a shift toward "in-person" social engineering at industry conferences.

### Other / Unknown
The **TeamPCP** campaign represents a new tier of cybercriminal capability, moving from open-source poisoning to the mass exfiltration of cloud secrets (T1528). By compromising tools like Trivy, they breached over 1,000 SaaS environments, including the European Commission. Additionally, **UAT-10608** automated the exploitation of the React2Shell vulnerability (T1190) to harvest thousands of SSH and Cloud keys (T1552.004) in under 24 hours.

## Targeted Sectors & Geographies
The victim landscape in April was dominated by **Government and Defense** institutions, particularly in the US and Israel, as part of the ongoing Middle Eastern conflict. ## Strategic Recommendations
1.  **Supply Chain Integrity:** Implement mandatory Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) and "soak times" for third-party dependencies. Adopt SLSA frameworks to verify the integrity of build pipelines.
2.  **AI Governance:** Establish strict guardrails for LLM agents (e.g., Amazon Bedrock Guardrails). Conduct security audits for AI service accounts and "reasoning engines" to prevent "Double Agent" scenarios.
3.  **Zero-Trust Identity:** Transition from legacy MFA to phishing-resistant FIDO2/WebAuthn to mitigate the surge in OAuth 2.0 and "Device Code" phishing (EvilTokens).
4.  **Resilience Planning:** In high-risk sectors (Health, Energy), prepare for "Wiper" scenarios by maintaining air-gapped, offline backups and multi-admin approval for MDM/Global Admin actions.

## Operational Recommendations
1.  **Patch Management:** Execute emergency patching for Next.js (CVE-2025-55182), Fortinet EMS (CVE-2026-35616), and Citrix NetScaler (CVE-2026-3055).
2.  **Secrets Rotation:** Perform a full rotation of AWS/GCP/Azure keys, SSH keys, and NPM tokens following any use of vulnerable versions of Axios (v1.14.1/0.30.4) or Trivy.
3.  **Endpoint Visibility:** Deploy specific EDR rules to detect "EDR Killer" behaviors (e.g., TechPowerUp driver loads or tech-specific driver misuse).
4.  **Cloud Auditing:** Monitor CloudTrail for unusual `STS:AssumeRole` calls and abnormal S3/Secret Manager enumeration associated with tools like TruffleHog.

## Conclusion
April 2026 demonstrated a fundamental shift in the threat landscape where the software supply chain is no longer just a vector for occasional espionage but a high-speed industrial pipe for mass cloud compromise. The convergence of kinetic conflict and cyber destruction, combined with the vulnerabilities of nascent AI infrastructures, requires a shift toward aggressive dependency management and identity-centric defense.

## Recommandations Stratégiques
1.  **Intégrité de la Supply Chain :** Mettre en œuvre des Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) obligatoires et des « délais d'observation » pour les dépendances tierces. Adopter les frameworks SLSA pour vérifier l'intégrité des pipelines de build.
2.  **Gouvernance de l'IA :** Établir des garde-fous stricts pour les agents LLM (ex: Amazon Bedrock Guardrails). Réaliser des audits de sécurité pour les comptes de service d'IA et les « moteurs de raisonnement » afin de prévenir les scénarios de « Double Agent ».
3.  **Identité Zero-Trust :** Transitionner du MFA traditionnel vers un MFA résistant au phishing (FIDO2/WebAuthn) pour atténuer la vague de phishing OAuth 2.0 et « Device Code » (EvilTokens).
4.  **Planification de la Résilience :** Dans les secteurs à haut risque (Santé, Énergie), se préparer aux scénarios de type « Wiper » en maintenant des sauvegardes hors ligne et isolées (air-gapped), et en exigeant une approbation multi-administrateurs pour les actions MDM ou d'administration globale.

## Recommandations Opérationnelles
1.  **Gestion des correctifs :** Exécuter des correctifs d'urgence pour Next.js (CVE-2025-55182), Fortinet EMS (CVE-2026-35616) et Citrix NetScaler (CVE-2026-3055).
2.  **Rotation des secrets :** Effectuer une rotation complète des clés AWS/GCP/Azure, des clés SSH et des jetons NPM suite à toute utilisation de versions vulnérables d'Axios (v1.14.1/0.30.4) ou de Trivy.
3.  **Visibilité des points de terminaison :** Déployer des règles EDR spécifiques pour détecter les comportements d'« EDR Killer » (ex: chargement de pilotes TechPowerUp ou détournement de pilotes techniques spécifiques).
4.  **Audit Cloud :** Surveiller CloudTrail pour détecter des appels `STS:AssumeRole` inhabituels et des énumérations anormales de S3/Secret Manager associées à des outils comme TruffleHog.

## Conclusion
Le mois d'avril 2026 a démontré un changement fondamental dans le paysage des menaces, où la chaîne d'approvisionnement logicielle n'est plus seulement un vecteur d'espionnage occasionnel, mais un pipeline industriel à grande vitesse pour la compromission massive du cloud. La convergence des conflits cinétiques et de la destruction cyber, combinée aux vulnérabilités des infrastructures d'IA naissantes, nécessite un virage vers une gestion agressive des dépendances et une défense centrée sur l'identité.
