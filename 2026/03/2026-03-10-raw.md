## Encrypted Client Hello: Ready for Prime Time&#x3f;, (Mon, Mar 9th)
Two RFCs, RFC 9848 and RFC 9849, were recently published regarding TLS Encrypted Client Hello (ECH). ECH aims to protect privacy by encrypting the TLS Client Hello message, which traditionally reveals sensitive information like the hostname. ECH leverages HTTPS DNS records to exchange encryption keys, and Cloudflare has already implemented it, allowing users to simply "flip a switch" to enable it.  Testing for ECH support involves examining the HTTPS record using tools like "dig," and blocking HTTPS records is a potential, albeit disruptive, mitigation strategy.



**Strategic Analysis & Recommendations for CISO/CERT:**

*   **Assess ECH Adoption:** Evaluate the potential benefits and risks of adopting ECH for your organization, considering its impact on compatibility and performance.
*   **Monitor ECH Deployment:** Track the adoption rate of ECH across the internet and within your organization's ecosystem.
*   **Evaluate Dependencies:** Understand the impact of ECH on third-party services and vendors.
*   **Consider Blocking HTTPS Records (with caution):**  While disruptive, blocking HTTPS records could be a mitigation strategy, but carefully weigh the impact on QUIC and other services.



**Operational Analysis & Recommendations for SOC/CERT:**

*   **Implement ECH Verification:** Utilize tools like `dig` and cloudflare-ech.com to verify ECH support on critical domains.
*   **Monitor DNS Records:**  Establish monitoring to detect changes or absence of the "ech=" attribute in HTTPS DNS records.
*   **Update DNS Resolution Tools:** Ensure your DNS resolution tools support HTTPS records to accurately test for ECH.
*   **Review Firewall Rules:**  If considering blocking HTTPS records, thoroughly test and document the impact on all services.
*   **Enhance TLS Handshake Monitoring:**  Implement or enhance monitoring of TLS handshake processes to detect anomalies related to ECH.
